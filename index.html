Improving Woodlot Management and Recreational Trail Planning through 3D Visualization

Many forested landscapes neighboring urban hubs are used both for wood harvesting and for recreational activities. In cases where public access is permitted for recreational use, private landowners often end up balancing the economic value of wood products and the demands of recreators using the land for activities. Communicating the necessity for harvesting and forest management to public stakeholders can be challenging. Providing accessible resources that the public can readily understand to demonstrate how harvesting income provides funding to upkeep recreational infrastructure is key to creating quality relationships between private woodlot owners and recreational stakeholders.

Case Study

The Larose Forest

In the early 19th century, eastern Ontario had a very active logging industry to supply lumber for the English navy and its shipbuilding industries. Once the old-growth forest had been depleted, loggers harvested smaller trees while farmers burned forests to clear land forcrops. By the early 20th century, the land in what is now the United Counties of Prescott and Russell (UCPR) was a blow-sand desert, called the Bourget desert. In the 1920s, the provincial agricultural representative for the area, Ferdinand Larose, put forward the idea of reforestation to combat desertification. The county bought the initial 490 hectares of desert-like land from farmers in 1928 and planted the first stands in the Larose Forest.

Since the initial purchase of the land, the county has continued to acquire land to its current size of 11,000 Ha. Replanting efforts focused on pine and spruce plantations resultng in a dominant coniferous species composition in the Larose Forest. The Larose Forest is still actively managed by the UCPR with a 20-year forest management plan approved in 2008. Although the primary mandate for the forest is land stewardship with sustainable timber harvesting, the Larose Forest has become a popular site for recreational activities where the forest is coveniently located 50 kilometers east of Canada's capital city, Ottawa. The forest now includes a multitude of recreational trails for hiking, mountain biking, horseback riding, snowshoeing, cross-country skiing, dog sledding, dirt biking, ATVing, hunting, and snowmobiling. The county recognizes that its forest harvesting must minimize the impact of the public’s use of its popular trails.

Our Approach

In this project, a web application will be constructed to communicate forest management operations to public stakeholders of the Larose Forest. Using recreational trail data provided by the UPCR, Light Detecting and Ranging (LiDAR) derived data, and the Larose Forest Management Plan, a web application will be created using Esri’s Experience Builder with embedded 3D Scenes and visualizations of the relationships between the harvesting plan and recreational trail systems. The aim of this project is to effectively communicate and visualize forestry management practices of privately owned woodlots that have recreational value to community stakeholders.
